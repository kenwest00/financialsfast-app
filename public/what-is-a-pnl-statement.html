What is a P&L statement?

Definition

A Profit & Loss (P&L) statement — also called an income statement — is a financial document that summarizes a business's revenue, costs, and expenses over a specific time period. It shows whether the business generated a profit or incurred a loss during that period. The "bottom line" of a P&L statement is net income: total revenue minus total expenses.

For small business owners applying for a loan, the P&L statement is one of the most important documents a lender will request. It tells the lender three critical things: how much money your business brings in, how much it spends to operate, and how much profit is left over to service debt.

A typical P&L statement covers 12 months, though some lenders accept shorter periods (3 to 6 months) for newer businesses or specific loan types. The statement period should match what your lender requires — always confirm before you start.

Why lenders require a P&L statement

Lenders use P&L statements to assess a borrower's ability to repay. Specifically, they evaluate:

  • Revenue trends: Is the business growing, stable, or declining? Consistent or increasing revenue signals a healthy business.
  • Expense ratios: What percentage of revenue goes to cost of goods sold, rent, payroll, and other operating expenses? High expense ratios may indicate thin margins.
  • Net income: Is there enough profit left after expenses to cover the proposed loan payments? This is the most critical metric.
  • Business stability: Do the numbers make sense for the stated business type? A landscaping company with $500,000 in software expenses would raise questions.

For SBA loans, the P&L is typically required alongside tax returns, a balance sheet, and a personal financial statement. For conventional business loans and lines of credit, the P&L may be the primary financial document alongside bank statements.

Standard P&L categories for small businesses

A well-organized P&L statement follows a multi-step income statement format. Each section builds on the previous one to arrive at net income.

Revenue

All income generated by business operations. This includes sales revenue, service income, Stripe or Square deposits, invoice payments, and any other business income. Non-revenue deposits — such as owner contributions, loan proceeds, and transfers from other accounts — are excluded from revenue.

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

Direct costs of producing the goods or services your business sells. For a product business, this includes materials, manufacturing labor, shipping, and packaging. For a service business, this may include subcontractor costs or direct project expenses. Many service businesses have zero or minimal COGS.

Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold. This is the profit your business earns before operating expenses. It's one of the first numbers a lender will look at because it shows the fundamental profitability of your business model.

Operating Expenses

The ongoing costs of running your business that are not directly tied to producing goods or services. Standard categories include:

Rent & Lease Payments

Office, retail, warehouse, or equipment lease payments.

Wages & Payroll

Employee salaries, payroll taxes, and payroll service fees (e.g., Gusto, ADP).

Utilities

Electric, gas, water, internet, and phone service for business locations.

Insurance

Business liability, property, workers' comp, and professional insurance premiums.

Marketing & Advertising

Google Ads, Meta Ads, print advertising, sponsorships, and promotional costs.

Software & Technology

SaaS subscriptions, hosting, domain registrations, and software licenses.

Vehicle Expenses

Fuel, maintenance, insurance, and lease payments for business vehicles. Prorated if the vehicle is used for both business and personal purposes.

Professional Services

Accounting, legal, consulting, and other professional fees.

Other Income & Expenses

Non-operating items such as interest income (from savings or investments) and interest expense (on loans or lines of credit). These are shown separately because they don't reflect the core business operations.

Memo Items (Excluded from P&L)

Certain transactions that appear on bank statements are not included in the P&L calculation but should be disclosed separately for transparency:

  • Owner draws and contributions — Money the owner takes out of or puts into the business. These are equity transactions, not income or expenses.
  • Loan principal payments — Only the interest portion of a loan payment is an expense. The principal portion is a balance sheet transaction.
  • Capital expenditures — Large asset purchases (equipment, vehicles) are typically capitalized and depreciated rather than expensed in the period of purchase.
  • Personal expenses — If using a mixed business/personal account, personal spending must be identified and excluded.
  • Transfers between accounts — Moving money between your own accounts is not revenue or expense.

Cash basis vs. accrual basis

There are two accounting methods for preparing a P&L statement. The method you use should be clearly stated on the document.

Cash Basis Accrual Basis
Revenue recorded When payment is received When earned (invoiced)
Expenses recorded When payment is made When incurred (billed)
Matches bank statements Yes — directly No — requires adjustments
Complexity Simpler for small businesses More complex, requires bookkeeping
Common for Most small businesses, sole proprietors Larger businesses, inventory-heavy companies
Lender acceptance Widely accepted Widely accepted

For small business owners creating a P&L from bank statements, cash basis is the natural choice because bank statements record when money actually moved — which is exactly what cash basis accounting tracks.

How to create a P&L from bank statements

If you don't use accounting software like QuickBooks or Xero, your bank statements are your primary financial record. Here's how to turn them into a P&L:

  1. Download your bank statements for the period your lender requires (typically 12 months). Use your primary business bank account. Most banks offer PDF statement downloads from their website.
  2. Identify revenue deposits. Go through each deposit and determine whether it's business revenue (customer payment, Stripe transfer, Square deposit) or non-revenue (owner contribution, loan proceeds, transfer from another account, refund).
  3. Categorize every expense. Assign each withdrawal to a P&L category: rent, payroll, materials, marketing, software, etc. If using a mixed account, identify and exclude personal expenses.
  4. Handle special items. Split loan payments into interest (expense) and principal (memo item). Prorate vehicle expenses if used for both business and personal. Identify capital expenditures versus operating expenses.
  5. Calculate subtotals. Revenue − COGS = Gross Profit. Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = Operating Income. Operating Income ± Other Items = Net Income.
  6. Format and document. Present in standard income statement format. Include notes explaining your methodology, estimates, and assumptions. Disclose that the statement is owner-prepared.

Owner-prepared vs. CPA-prepared financial statements

There's a common misconception that loan applications require CPA-prepared financial statements. In most cases, they don't.

Owner-prepared financial statements are financial documents created by the business owner rather than by a licensed CPA or accounting firm. They are not audited, reviewed, or compiled under professional accounting standards. However, they are widely accepted for small business loan applications because most small businesses don't maintain CPA-prepared financials.

The key differences:

  • CPA-prepared: A licensed accountant prepares and signs the statement. Comes with a CPA's professional assurance. Costs $400–$2,000. Takes 1–2 weeks. Required for some larger loans and specific lender policies.
  • Owner-prepared: The business owner creates the statement using their own records. No professional assurance attached. Can be created for free, or using tools like accounting software or AI-powered generators. Accepted by most lenders for small business loans, including many SBA loans.

Using software (QuickBooks, Xero, or AI tools like Financials Fast) to generate your P&L does not make it CPA-prepared. The statement should clearly disclose that it is owner-prepared and describe the methodology used. This is standard practice and not a negative — it simply sets the correct expectation for the lender.

Common mistakes to avoid

When creating a P&L statement for a loan application, avoid these errors that can delay your application or raise red flags with lenders:

  • Counting transfers as revenue. Moving money between your own accounts is not income. Transfers from savings, other business accounts, or credit card payments should be excluded from revenue.
  • Including personal expenses. If your business runs through a personal account, every personal purchase must be identified and excluded. A $200 Netflix and grocery run in the middle of business expenses will undermine credibility.
  • Expensing loan principal. Only the interest portion of a loan payment is an expense. The principal is a balance sheet transaction. Including full loan payments as expenses understates your net income.
  • Missing the period your lender needs. If your lender requires 12 months and you submit 6, they'll ask you to redo it. Confirm the required period before you start.
  • No methodology notes. A P&L without notes explaining how transactions were classified, what estimates were made, and what accounting basis was used will face more scrutiny than one with clear documentation.
  • Inconsistent categories. If you call it "Advertising" in one month and "Marketing" in another, the statement looks sloppy. Use consistent category names throughout.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a CPA to create a P&L statement for a loan?

No. Most lenders accept owner-prepared P&L statements for small business loans, including SBA loans. A CPA-prepared statement carries additional credibility but is not required for most applications. If your lender specifically requires a CPA-reviewed statement, your owner-prepared P&L can serve as the starting document for the CPA, saving them time and you money.

What is the difference between a P&L and a balance sheet?

A P&L statement shows income and expenses over a period of time (like a movie — what happened during the year). A balance sheet shows assets, liabilities, and equity at a single point in time (like a photograph — what you own and owe right now). Lenders may request both, but the P&L is the primary document for assessing income and repayment ability.

How many months of P&L does a lender need?

Most lenders require 12 months. Some accept 3 to 6 months for newer businesses, bridge loans, or specific loan products. SBA loans typically require 12 months. Always confirm with your lender before preparing the statement.

Can I create a P&L from bank statements?

Yes. Bank statements contain every deposit and withdrawal, which represent your revenue and expenses on a cash basis. The process involves identifying revenue deposits, categorizing expenses, separating business from personal transactions, handling special items (loan splits, vehicle proration), and formatting the results into a standard income statement. This can be done manually, by a CPA, or using AI-powered tools like Financials Fast.

What if my business and personal expenses are in the same account?

This is common for sole proprietors and single-member LLCs. You'll need to go through each transaction and identify whether it's business or personal. Business expenses go on the P&L; personal expenses are excluded. The P&L should include a note disclosing that the account contains mixed transactions and explaining how business expenses were identified.

How accurate does a P&L need to be for a loan application?

It needs to be materially accurate — meaning the overall financial picture should be correct even if a few small transactions are miscategorized. A $50 marketing expense categorized as "office supplies" won't matter. Counting a $10,000 owner contribution as revenue will. Lenders look at the big picture: total revenue, major expense categories, and net income. Clear notes explaining your methodology add credibility.
